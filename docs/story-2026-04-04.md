## Story Structure

### Opening Hook

How many times this week did you ask a second model?

You have access to Claude, Gemini, Codex — you may even be paying for more than one. But the tab switch and the copy-paste never happen. So every answer you ship comes from one model's opinion.

One of them has already been wrong in a way you haven't caught yet.

The second opinion exists. The workflow to get it doesn't.

**Roundtable is that workflow.**

### Core Narrative

---

**You know the failure mode.**

Not the obvious hallucination. The dangerous one: correct pattern, correct library, wrong detail. A parameter name that changed two versions ago. A concurrency fix that looks elegant and quietly reintroduces a race condition. An infrastructure block that reads like real documentation but doesn't exist.

It compiles. It passes your smell test. It ships. You find out at 2am.

You cross-check sometimes. Just not often enough to catch the subtle ones — because cross-checking means re-establishing context in another terminal, copy-pasting a prompt, waiting, and mentally diffing two walls of prose. So you only do it for decisions you *already* think are risky. The ones that burn you are the ones you didn't think were risky.

**Roundtable removes that friction.** It's an MCP server that sends your prompt to every CLI you have — in parallel — and returns structured JSON with all their responses. One tool call from inside your existing agent. It uses the CLIs already in your PATH, already authenticated. Runs locally. Nothing stored, nothing forwarded — prompts go directly to your CLIs.

You can run the same CLI with different models in a single dispatch. Claude with Opus for the architecture review, Claude with Sonnet for the quick sanity check. Gemini for the edge cases. Codex for an independent take. Compose your own panel.

---

**When they agree, that's useful triage** — not proof, but a strong signal you're on the right track.

**When they disagree, that's the real value.** Disagreement surfaces tradeoffs you would have missed, edge cases one model sees and another doesn't, or a hallucination the others don't share. You don't need three models to be right. You need them to be *different enough* to catch each other.

Here's what that looks like:

```json
{
  "claude": { "status": "ok", "response": "Use a message queue — decouples the producer..." },
  "gemini": { "status": "ok", "response": "Use a message queue with dead-letter handling..." },
  "codex":  { "status": "ok", "response": "A cron job is simpler here — the volume doesn't justify a queue..." }
}
```

Two models agree on the queue. One says it's overengineered. That disagreement is worth more than any single answer.

---

**This isn't demoware.**

Each dispatch gets its own supervision tree — if one CLI hangs, the others still return. Process groups are killed atomically on shutdown. No orphaned subprocesses eating your RAM. Cross-platform: Linux, macOS, Windows.

Selective dispatch means you control cost. Route architecture decisions to the heavy models. Route boilerplate to the fast ones. The `agents` parameter takes a JSON array — pick exactly who sits at the table.

---

**Try it.**

Have your favorite agent read [INSTALL.md](../INSTALL.md) — it covers Claude Code, Codex, OpenCode, and any MCP client.

Your existing CLIs. Your existing auth. Then ask all of them the question you were about to ask just one.

See where they disagree. That's where you should look twice.

## Story Elements Analysis

### Character/Voice

The engineer is the hero. Roundtable is the tool they were already smart enough to want. Tone is peer-to-peer — one engineer telling another about something that solved a real problem. No marketing voice. No superlatives.

### Conflict/Tension

Single-model trust vs. the friction of verification. The reader knows they should cross-check but the workflow makes it practically impossible. The tension resolves not through discipline but through removing the friction entirely.

### Transformation/Change

From "I trust one model and hope for the best" to "I hear all my models and ship when they converge." The transformation is behavioral — the tool changes the default, not the engineer's character.

### Emotional Touchpoints

- The 2am production incident caused by a confident hallucination
- The realization you're paying for opinions you never hear
- The first time you see three models disagree and realize you would have shipped the wrong one

### Key Messages

1. You may have access to multiple AI agents — use them together
2. The friction of cross-checking is the real enemy, not laziness
3. Agreement is triage; disagreement is where tradeoffs hide
4. One tool call replaces three terminals — local, nothing stored
5. Built on real infrastructure, not duct tape

### Audience Considerations

- Engineers in this audience are skeptical of marketing language — keep it peer-to-peer
- They care about how it works, not just what it does — the OTP/supervision detail earns trust
- They will try it only if setup is under 5 minutes — emphasize zero-config
- They respect tools that solve problems the author actually had — dogfooding credibility matters

### Tone and Voice Notes

- First person plural or second person ("you") — never third person
- Short sentences. Sentence fragments are fine.
- Technical details are credibility, not jargon — include them but keep them precise
- No superlatives ("revolutionary", "game-changing") — let the reader decide
- Mild provocation is good ("one of them has already been wrong") — lecturing is bad
